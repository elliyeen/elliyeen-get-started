# Agent 18 — Competitive Advantage Audit

**Frameworks:** McKinsey Digital · David Ogilvy · Michael Porter (Competitive Strategy)
**Compensation driver:** A company without a moat competes on price until margins disappear.

---

## Mission

Identify what this company has that competitors cannot easily replicate, what it is currently positioning as a differentiator that competitors already match, and what it should own before someone else does.

Ogilvy: "Unless your advertising is built on a BIG IDEA, it will pass like a ship in the night."

The Big Idea is almost always tied to the competitive moat. If the moat is invisible, the idea is not big enough.

---

- Does it occupy a unique position, or is it in a cluster with competitors?
- Is it trying to compete on the same dimensions as everyone else?

## The Four Types of Competitive Moat

### 1. Credential Moat
Something the founder or team has that competitors cannot claim.
Examples: Specific license, clinical credential, years in a niche, industry recognition.

- Does this company have a credential moat?
- Is it communicated on the website?

### 2. Process Moat
A named, specific way of doing the work that competitors have not published.
Examples: A proprietary 5-step intake process, a branded assessment methodology.

- Does this company have a named process?
- Is it described with enough specificity to be memorable and uncopyable?

### 3. Network Moat
Relationships, referral sources, community trust, institutional partnerships.
Examples: Hospital discharge coordinators, community organizations, professional associations.

- Is this moat visible on the website?
- Would a prospect know about it?

### 4. Story Moat
The founder origin story, the reason the company exists, the specific moment that created the mission.
Examples: A founder who became a CNA after a family experience and built the company they wish had existed.

- Is the story specific?
- Is it told?
- Could a competitor tell the same story?

---

## The Generic Audit

Review every positioning claim on the website.

For each claim, ask: "Could a competitor put this on their homepage without lying?"

If yes, it is generic. Flag it.

Generic claims to identify:
- "We are dedicated to providing exceptional service."
- "Our team is passionate about helping our clients."
- "We offer personalized care."
- "We are committed to your success."

Every generic claim should be replaced with a specific, ownable statement.

---

## The Preemptive Claim Opportunity

Hopkins: "A preemptive claim is not what only you do — it is what you say first."

What does this company do that every competitor also does, but no competitor has claimed publicly?

This is the highest-value copy on the website. It makes a standard practice sound like a proprietary standard.

Identify it. Claim it.

---

## Switching Cost Analysis

What makes a customer unlikely to leave once they choose this company?

- Relationship depth (they know us, we know them)
- Accumulated trust and track record
- Integration into their life or operations
- Sunk cost of transition

Does the website communicate these switching costs as benefits?
