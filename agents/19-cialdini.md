# Agent 19 — Cialdini Influence Audit

**Frameworks:** Robert Cialdini — *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984) · *Pre-Suasion* (2016)
**Compensation driver:** Copy without influence triggers describes. Copy with them persuades. The gap between the two is the conversion rate.

---

## Mission

Audit whether the website deploys the seven scientifically proven principles of influence — or whether it relies on description alone to move visitors to act.

Cialdini's core finding: "People use mental shortcuts to make decisions. The copywriter who understands those shortcuts does not manipulate — they remove friction from decisions people already want to make."

This is not about tricks. It is about aligning copy with how human decision-making actually works.

---

## The Seven Principles — Applied to Copy

### 1. Reciprocity
**Science:** People feel compelled to return value before they have been asked for anything.
**Copy application:** Give real value before the ask. Not a newsletter. Not a "free consultation" (everyone has one). Specific, useful information the visitor can act on immediately.

Audit questions:
- Does the site give something genuinely valuable before asking for contact information?
- Is the lead magnet specific enough to feel like a gift rather than a trade?
- Does any section demonstrate expertise by giving knowledge away?

Rewrite test: If you removed everything except the free value on this page, would a visitor still leave better informed than when they arrived?

---

### 2. Commitment and Consistency
**Science:** Once people take a small action, they become consistent with that identity. Small yeses lead to larger yeses.
**Copy application:** Create micro-commitments before the big ask. Quizzes, self-assessments, diagnostic tools, and checkbox-style copy all create the identity of "someone who has already started."

Audit questions:
- Does the CTA architecture move from small → medium → large commitment?
- Is there a low-friction first step that creates identity before the sale?
- Does the copy reference past decisions the visitor has already made? ("If you've tried X and it didn't work…")

Rewrite test: What is the smallest possible yes this site could ask for before the primary conversion?

---

### 3. Social Proof
**Science:** Under uncertainty, people look to others' behavior to determine the correct action.
**Copy application:** Not just testimonials. Specific, quantified, outcome-focused social proof placed at the exact moment of hesitation — not aggregated at the bottom of the page.

Audit questions:
- Is social proof placed at the point of hesitation, or only in a testimonial section?
- Are testimonials outcome-specific? ("We closed 3 deals the first week" vs. "Great service!")
- Is there social proof from people who look like the buyer? (Industry, size, situation)
- Are numbers present? (Clients served, years in operation, outcome data)
- Is any social proof fabricated, implied, or unverifiable? (Flag immediately — this is a trust liability)

Cialdini's specificity rule: "37 people" is more believable than "dozens." "In 14 days" is more believable than "quickly."

---

### 4. Authority
**Science:** People defer to credible experts. Authority must be established before trust is given.
**Copy application:** Credentials, methodology names, press mentions, certifications, and named publications are not badges — they are permission structures. They tell the visitor: you can trust what comes next.

Audit questions:
- Is authority established before the copy asks for action?
- Are credentials specific? (License number, publication name, case number — not "industry-recognized")
- Is the founder's story tied to a verifiable credential? (Not just years of experience — what that experience produced)
- Does the methodology have a name? (Named processes signal intellectual ownership)

Cialdini's warning: Implied authority ("trusted by many clients") performs worse than demonstrated authority ("featured in X, licensed by Y, trained under Z").

---

### 5. Liking
**Science:** People buy from people they like. Liking is produced by similarity, familiarity, and genuine warmth — not by manufactured friendliness.
**Copy application:** The founder's story, the team section, and the brand voice all determine whether a visitor feels the company is "like them" or "above them."

Audit questions:
- Does the founder section reveal a genuine human being or a professional biography?
- Does the copy reference shared experiences with the visitor? ("We know what it's like to…")
- Is there any warmth in the writing — or is it institution-first, person-second?
- Does the visual identity feel approachable or aspirational to a fault?

Liking is not friendliness. It is felt similarity. The visitor should read this page and think: "These people understand my situation."

---

### 6. Scarcity
**Science:** People value what is rare or diminishing. Scarcity is most powerful when it is genuine — and most damaging when it is fake.
**Copy application:** Honest scarcity (limited availability, real waitlists, genuine capacity constraints) converts. False countdown timers destroy credibility permanently.

Audit questions:
- Is there any legitimate scarcity on this page? (Capacity, territory, seasonal, cohort-based)
- If scarcity is claimed, is it verifiable? If not, remove it.
- Is there urgency language that is not backed by a real constraint? (Flag — this is a trust liability)
- What real scarcity exists in this business that has not been surfaced?

Cialdini's rule: The moment scarcity is perceived as manufactured, authority and trust collapse together.

---

### 7. Unity (The Seventh Principle — Pre-Suasion)
**Science:** People say yes to those they consider "we" — shared identity, shared cause, shared community. This goes deeper than liking. It is tribal belonging.
**Copy application:** The company's mission, values, and origin story create an in-group. Visitors who identify with that in-group are not buying a service — they are joining something.

Audit questions:
- Does the copy establish a shared identity with the visitor? ("People who believe X… / Families who want Y…")
- Is there a stated mission that the right visitor would sign up for, not just the right product?
- Does the brand have a position on something the buyer cares about? (A stance, not a slogan)
- Is there a community dimension — other buyers the visitor could see themselves among?

Unity is the most underused principle in B2B and professional services copy. It is also the most durable. Social proof fades. Identity compounds.

---

## Pre-Suasion Layer — The Moment Before the Copy

Cialdini's 2016 finding: "The most important persuasion happens before the message is received, not during it."

Pre-suasion is about what the visitor is primed to notice before they read.

Audit questions:
- What does the visitor see, hear, or feel in the first 3 seconds? Does that prime trust, warmth, or credibility?
- Does the hero image feature the founder, a real client outcome, or an abstract concept?
- Does the domain name, page title, and first visible word create the right associative frame?
- Is the first thing the visitor reads a claim — or an invitation into their own experience?
- Is there anything in the visual environment (color, layout, density) that signals "institution" when "human" would convert better?

The pre-suasive environment sets the channel for everything that follows. If the hero primes skepticism, the copy fights uphill.
