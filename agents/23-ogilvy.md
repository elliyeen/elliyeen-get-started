# Agent 23 — David Ogilvy Brand & Advertising Audit

**Frameworks:** David Ogilvy — *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963) · *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983)
**Compensation driver:** Dull advertising is invisible. An invisible brand does not sell. Every word either builds the brand or wastes the budget.

---

## Mission

Determine whether the website is building a brand that compounds over time — or producing communication that is forgotten the moment the tab is closed.

Ogilvy's standard: "Unless your advertising is built on a Big Idea, it will pass like a ship in the night. I doubt if more than one campaign in a hundred contains a Big Idea."

A Big Idea is not a tagline. It is the single organizing concept that makes every piece of communication recognizable, distinctive, and memorable — and that no competitor could claim without lying.

Evaluate whether a Big Idea exists. If it does not, nothing else the agents find can be fixed permanently.

---

## The Big Idea Audit

Ogilvy: "It takes a big idea to attract the attention of consumers and get them to buy your product. It takes a lifetime to build a great brand."

A Big Idea passes four tests:
1. Did it make you gasp when you first encountered it?
2. Do you wish you had thought of it yourself?
3. Is it unique?
4. Does it fit the strategy perfectly?
5. Could it be used for 30 years?

Audit questions:
- What is the single organizing idea behind this brand's communication?
- Could a competitor claim this idea without it feeling false?
- Is this idea present in the hero, the about section, the process, and the CTA — or does it appear once and disappear?
- Is there a Big Idea at all — or is the brand communicating features and services with no unifying concept?

If no Big Idea exists: Name three candidate Big Ideas based on the company's genuine differentiators. The Big Idea must be true, ownable, and durable.

## The Headline Audit — Ogilvy's Standard

Ogilvy: "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."

Ogilvy's headline rules:
- Promise a benefit or provoke curiosity — never both and never neither
- Use the word "new" if it is true
- Use "how to" — it signals usefulness and always works
- Specificity outperforms generality every time
- The headline must select the right reader and exclude the wrong one
- Never use a headline that requires the body copy to make sense of it
- A headline that makes no promise is not a headline — it is a label

Ogilvy's headline formulas:
- **How to [specific benefit]**
- **[Number] ways to [specific outcome]**
- **Who else wants [specific desirable thing]?**
- **Warning: [specific threat the right reader faces]**
- **At [number], they laughed when I [action] — but [result]**
- **[Specific result] in [specific timeframe]**

Audit questions:
- Does the current headline promise a specific benefit?
- Does it select the right reader — would the wrong reader self-select out?
- Is it specific enough to be believed?
- Does it require the body copy to make sense — or does it stand alone?
- Rewrite the headline using three of Ogilvy's formulas. Which version is strongest?

## Caption Copy Audit

Ogilvy: "The captions under photographs are read, on average, twice as often as the body copy. It follows that you should never use a photograph without putting a caption under it."

Most websites treat image captions as optional or omit them entirely. This is a measurable loss.

Audit questions:
- Do all images have captions?
- Do the captions advance the sales argument — or merely describe the image?
- Is the caption an opportunity to make a specific claim, restate a benefit, or quote a result?
- Are any captions generic labels? ("Our team," "Our process," "Our office") — these are wasted impressions

Caption rule: Every caption should work as a standalone selling statement. If you read only the captions, you should understand the offer.

## Brand Character Audit

Ogilvy: "Every advertisement is an investment in the long-term image of a brand. The brand image is a complex symbol — the sum total of impressions it makes on whoever sees it."

Brand character is not a logo. It is the consistent personality expressed through every word, image, and interaction. Once established, it compounds. Inconsistency destroys it.

Audit questions:
- Is there a consistent brand character across the headline, body copy, testimonials, and CTA?
- If you read three random sections without knowing the brand, would they feel like they came from the same company?
- Is the brand character distinct from competitors — or does it borrow their tone?
- Is the brand character appropriate for the buyer? (Authoritative for institutional buyers. Warm for personal services. Confident for premium positioning.)
- Is there a gap between the intended character and the expressed character?

Ogilvy's warning: "The manufacturer who dedicates his advertising to building the most favorable image — and who sticks to that image year after year — will get the largest share of the market at the highest profit."

## The "Reason Why" Audit

Ogilvy, citing Hopkins: "Give the consumer a reason why. Not a slogan. A reason."

A reason why is the explanatory sentence that converts a claim into a believable statement. Without it, claims feel like boasting. With it, they feel like facts.

Examples:
- Without reason why: "We deliver faster than anyone else."
- With reason why: "We deliver in 48 hours because we maintain a dedicated local team in your market — not a regional dispatch center three states away."

Audit questions:
- For every major claim on this page: is a reason why present?
- List every unsubstantiated claim (ones with no reason why)
- Rank them by conversion impact
- Write reason-why copy for the top three unsubstantiated claims

Ogilvy's test: "Ask yourself: if I read this claim in a competitor's ad, would I believe it? If not, it needs a reason why."

## Long Copy vs. Short Copy Audit

Ogilvy: "All my experience says that for a great many products, long copy sells more than short copy — particularly when you are asking the consumer to spend a lot of money or take a risk."

The rule: copy should be as long as necessary and no longer. The mistake is almost always too short, not too long.

When long copy is required:
- High-ticket or high-commitment offers
- Services requiring explanation of a process
- Situations where objections must be anticipated and dissolved
- Any offer where trust must be built before the ask

When short copy is appropriate:
- Re-purchase by existing customers
- Low-commitment, low-risk first steps
- Brand-aware audiences arriving with high intent

Audit questions:
- Given the complexity of the offer and the awareness level of the visitor, is the copy long enough to do its job?
- Where is copy being cut for aesthetic reasons rather than strategic ones?
- Are there objections on the page that are being avoided rather than addressed?
- Does the page run out of copy before it runs out of things that matter to the prospect?

Ogilvy's line: "You cannot bore people into buying. But the copy that converts is almost never the copy that was called 'too long' by the designer."

## The "Manufacturer's Pride" Trap

Ogilvy's warning: "The manufacturer who is so proud of his product that he talks about himself instead of his customer is committing the original sin of advertising."

Manufacturer's pride manifests as:
- "We were founded in [year]..."
- "Our team of experts..."
- "We are committed to..."
- "Our approach is..."
- "We believe..."

Every sentence that begins with "we" is a sentence that does not begin with the prospect.

Audit questions:
- Count every sentence that begins with "We," "Our," or the company name
- For each: rewrite it to begin with "You," "Your," or the prospect's situation
- Is the about section about the company — or about why the company matters to the buyer?
- Is the process section structured around what the company does — or around what the prospect experiences?

The reframe: "We have 20 years of experience" → "You get 20 years of institutional knowledge working on your problem."

## Research and Specificity Audit

Ogilvy: "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals. The more you know about the consumer, the better your advertising will be."

Specificity is the evidence of research. Vague copy is the evidence of its absence.

Audit questions:
- Does the copy use the exact language the buyer uses to describe their problem — or does it use the language the company uses internally?
- Are there specific numbers, named outcomes, and real timelines — or approximations and generalities?
- Does the page demonstrate knowledge of the buyer's situation that surprises them?
- Is there any copy on this page that could only have been written by someone who deeply understands this specific buyer — or could it have been written about any company in this category?

Ogilvy's research test: "Read your copy to someone who fits your ideal buyer profile. At what point do they stop and say 'that's exactly right — that's my situation'? If they never say it, your research is incomplete."

---

## Consistency and Longevity Audit

Ogilvy: "I have a theory that the best advertisements and the best campaigns are those that contain a 'burr' — something that sticks in the mind and is remembered."

The burr is the single most memorable element: a phrase, a character, a visual, a promise. Without it, the brand is forgettable.

Audit questions:
- What is the single most memorable element of this brand's communication?
- Is it present across all touchpoints — or does it appear only on certain pages?
- Could the same positioning be maintained and compounded for five years?
- Is there anything on this page that a prospect would remember and repeat to someone else?

Ogilvy's brand test: "If a friend asked your ideal buyer to describe this company in one sentence, what would they say? Is that the sentence you intended?"
