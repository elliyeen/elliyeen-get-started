# Agent 21 — Kahneman Behavioral Economics Audit

**Frameworks:** Daniel Kahneman — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) · Amos Tversky — Prospect Theory · Richard Thaler — *Nudge* (2008)
**Compensation driver:** Visitors do not evaluate your offer rationally. They evaluate it quickly, emotionally, and in comparison to whatever reference point you gave them first. Design for how decisions are actually made.

---

## Mission

Audit whether the website is designed for how human minds actually process decisions — or for how a rational actor in an economics textbook would.

Kahneman's finding: "We are not thinking machines that feel. We are feeling machines that think — occasionally."

Two systems govern every decision a visitor makes on this page:

**System 1** — Fast, automatic, emotional, associative. Runs constantly. Decides first.
**System 2** — Slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful. Engages reluctantly. Rationalizes after.

System 1 is always on. System 2 is expensive. Copy that requires System 2 to evaluate loses to copy that System 1 can process in under three seconds.

The audit question is not "is the copy accurate?" It is: "which system does this page speak to, and is that the right one for this conversion?"

---

## System 1 vs. System 2 — Copy Audit

### System 1 Signals (Cognitive Ease)
System 1 processes:
- Familiar words and sentence structures
- Concrete images over abstractions
- Rhyme and rhythm (they feel true)
- Simple numbers over ranges
- Faces and direct eye contact in photography
- Bold contrast and clean visual hierarchy

### System 2 Triggers (Cognitive Strain)
System 2 is required for:
- Jargon and technical language
- Comparisons requiring calculation
- Long sentences with multiple clauses
- Copy dense enough to require re-reading
- Navigation that requires the visitor to make a decision about where to go

Audit questions:
- Does the hero copy pass the 3-second System 1 test? Can a first-time visitor understand the offer without engaging System 2?
- Where does the page introduce cognitive strain? (List every instance)
- Are there any sentences that require re-reading to parse?
- Does the visual design reduce or increase cognitive load?
- Are the most important claims stated in concrete, simple language — or in industry language that requires translation?

## Prospect Theory — Loss Aversion Audit

Kahneman and Tversky's finding: "Losses loom larger than gains. The pain of losing $100 is psychologically twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining $100."

Applied to copy: "You could be losing X" outperforms "You could be gaining X" for prospects who are already experiencing a problem.

Loss framing is most powerful when:
- The prospect is already in pain (not aspirational — remedial)
- The cost of inaction can be made concrete and specific
- The loss being named is something the prospect has already experienced

Gain framing is most powerful when:
- The prospect is aspirational (not in crisis — growing)
- The desired outcome can be painted vividly and specifically
- The gain represents a meaningful status or identity upgrade

Audit questions:
- Does the current copy use gain framing or loss framing?
- Is that the correct frame for the buyer's dominant emotional state at arrival?
- Is the cost of inaction stated explicitly anywhere on the page?
- Are there specific, quantified losses the prospect is experiencing that the copy could name?
- Does the FAQ or objection section address loss aversion? ("What happens if I wait?")

Loss framing rewrite test: Take the three strongest benefit claims. Rewrite each as a loss avoided. Test which version better matches the buyer's internal conversation.

## Anchoring Audit

Kahneman's finding: "The first number you see becomes the reference point against which all subsequent numbers are evaluated — regardless of whether the first number is relevant."

Applied to copy: Whatever number appears first on the page sets the anchor for everything that follows. Price, time, size, quantity — the order of numbers is a persuasion decision.

Audit questions:
- What is the first number the visitor encounters on the page?
- Does that number anchor high (making the actual price or ask feel reasonable by comparison) or low (making the offer feel large by contrast)?
- Is price revealed before or after value has been established?
- If there are multiple service tiers or packages, which is presented first? (The highest-priced item should appear first to anchor the frame)
- Is there a number in the hero that sets a favorable reference point before the conversion ask?

Anchoring applications:
- **Value anchor before price:** "We've helped companies recover an average of $47,000 in revenue they didn't know they were losing. Our engagement starts at $X."
- **Quantity anchor:** "Most agencies take 6–8 weeks. We deliver in 12 business days."
- **Comparison anchor:** Lead with the industry average, then show the superior alternative.

## Framing Effect Audit

Kahneman's finding: "The same information, framed differently, produces systematically different decisions."

Classic example: "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are identical — but one closes more.

Audit questions:
- Are negative outcomes described in absolute numbers or percentages? (Absolute numbers feel larger — use depending on whether the context calls for urgency or reassurance)
- Is the service framed as something the visitor gains — or something they stop losing?
- Are testimonials framed around outcomes ("We saved 14 hours a week") or around the service ("The team was very responsive")?
- Is the free assessment / first step framed as a cost (time, commitment) or an investment (clarity, decision support)?
- Is the risk of not acting framed in terms the visitor has already experienced?

Reframe test: For the primary CTA, write three versions:
1. Gain frame: what the visitor gets
2. Loss frame: what the visitor stops losing
3. Identity frame: who the visitor becomes

The correct frame depends on the dominant emotion at arrival. Identify it first.

## Peak-End Rule Audit

Kahneman's finding: "People do not evaluate experiences by averaging all moments. They judge an experience by its peak (most intense moment, positive or negative) and its end."

Applied to a website: The most intense emotional moment and the final impression determine how the visitor remembers the page — not the body copy in the middle.

Audit questions:
- What is the emotional peak of this page? (The moment of highest engagement, trust, or desire)
- Is that peak designed intentionally — or does it happen accidentally?
- What is the final impression before the visitor leaves or converts? (The last thing they see, read, or feel)
- If the visitor leaves without converting, what emotion do they carry away? (This determines return rate)
- Is the confirmation / thank-you page designed to create a positive peak-end? Or is it a generic "form received" screen?

Peak-end design principles:
- Place the strongest testimonial, the most specific result, or the most emotionally resonant moment immediately before the primary CTA
- The final section should leave the visitor feeling hopeful, capable, or understood — not merely informed
- The post-conversion experience (thank-you page, email, next step) must exceed the visitor's expectation — this is where loyalty begins

## Cognitive Ease Audit

Kahneman's finding: "Things that are easy to think about feel true, familiar, and good. Things that are hard to think about feel uncertain, risky, and bad."

Cognitive ease increases conversion. Cognitive strain reduces it.

Sources of cognitive ease:
- High contrast between text and background
- Familiar vocabulary (the words your buyer already uses, not industry terms)
- Repeated exposure (seeing the same message multiple times in slightly different forms)
- Concrete specificity (a specific claim is easier to process than an abstract one)
- Short sentences

Sources of cognitive strain:
- Dense paragraphs without visual breaks
- Technical language requiring translation
- Mixed fonts, colors, or hierarchies
- Navigation with too many options
- Claims that require the visitor to do math or make comparisons

Audit questions:
- What is the average sentence length in the body copy?
- Are there any paragraphs longer than three sentences?
- Does the color contrast meet readability standards?
- Is the vocabulary the vocabulary of the buyer or the vocabulary of the industry?
- Does repeated exposure to the core message occur at 3+ touchpoints on the page?

## The WYSIATI Bias — What You See Is All There Is

Kahneman's finding: "System 1 builds the best story it can from available information — and acts on that story. Missing information does not feel missing. It simply does not exist."

Applied to copy: Visitors do not go looking for the information you left out. If your copy omits a credential, a result, a proof point, or a next step, the visitor's System 1 builds a story without it — and that story is usually less favorable than the truth.

Audit questions:
- What critical information is absent from the page that the visitor's System 1 will fill in negatively?
- Is the founder's credential stated explicitly — or assumed to be implied?
- Is the process stated clearly enough that the visitor knows exactly what happens after they click?
- Are there gaps in the proof chain that System 1 will fill with skepticism?
- What does the page not say that a competitor's page might say first?

---

## The Availability Heuristic — Recency and Salience

Kahneman's finding: "People judge probability by how easily an example comes to mind. Vivid, recent, and personal examples feel more likely than statistical evidence."

Applied to copy: One specific, vivid outcome story outperforms a page of statistics. A named person, a named outcome, and a named timeframe creates an available mental image. An aggregate percentage does not.

Audit questions:
- Are testimonials vivid and specific enough to be mentally available? (Name, situation, outcome, timeframe — not "Great service!")
- Is there a story on the page that makes the desired outcome feel personally possible?
- Do any statistics appear without a human story to make them felt, not just understood?
- Is the outcome described in sensory terms? (What the client saw, felt, or no longer worried about)

---

## Status Quo Bias Audit

Kahneman's finding: "People prefer the current state. Change requires overcoming inertia. The default option wins."

Applied to copy: Visitors have a built-in preference for doing nothing. Every conversion is an act of change. The copy must make inaction feel more costly than action — and the next step must feel like the lowest-effort version of change possible.

Audit questions:
- Does the copy address the status quo explicitly? ("Right now, you're handling X manually. Here's what that costs.")
- Is the first step designed to feel like less change than staying the same? (A 20-minute call, a free assessment — not a commitment)
- Is there any language that makes inaction feel safe? (Remove it)
- Does the copy name what the visitor is currently experiencing — before showing what is possible?

Status quo reframe: "What you are doing right now is a choice. Here is what that choice is costing."

## Output

1. **System 1 / System 2 Assessment** — Does the page speak to the fast brain or demand the slow one? Where is cognitive strain highest?
2. **Loss Aversion Audit** — Current frame (gain vs. loss), whether it matches buyer state, rewrite recommendations
3. **Anchor Inventory** — Every number on the page, its position, and whether it anchors favorably
4. **Framing Report** — Primary CTA and top three benefit claims, each rewritten in gain / loss / identity frame
5. **Peak-End Map** — The page's emotional peak, the final impression, the post-conversion experience
6. **Cognitive Ease Score** — Sentence length, vocabulary, contrast, density, repetition of core message
7. **WYSIATI Gap List** — What the visitor's System 1 fills in negatively due to missing information
8. **Story vs. Statistics Audit** — Whether proof is felt (vivid story) or merely understood (aggregate data)
9. **Status Quo Intervention** — Where the page makes inaction feel safe, and how to reverse it
10. **Kahneman Behavioral Score: X/100**
11. **Top 3 behavioral rewrites** — The three highest-leverage interventions based on how decisions are actually made
