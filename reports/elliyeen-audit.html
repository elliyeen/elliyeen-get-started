Before vs. After — Copy Rewrites

1. Hero Subheadline

Current
"The answer. The roadmap. The copy already written."
Rewrite
"Most audits tell you what's wrong. We hand you the fixed version — copy rewrites, UX changes, a week-by-week plan."

Why: The current version is abstract ("the answer" to what?). The rewrite makes explicit what is delivered and names the gap competitors don't close.

2. Hero CTA — Remove the secondary prompt

Current
Primary: "Get your free diagnosis"
Secondary: "See how it works →"
Rewrite
Primary: "Get your free diagnosis"
(Remove secondary CTA entirely. The process section is one scroll away.)

3. Hero Credibility Line — Activate the scarcity

Current
"Three specific findings. Delivered within 48 hours. We run 4 per week. No pitch."
Rewrite
"Three specific findings within 48 hours. We run 4 diagnostics per week — 2 slots available. No pitch, no commitment."

Why: "2 slots available" turns a credibility detail into a genuine conversion lever. Update weekly. Cialdini's scarcity principle applied honestly.

4. AI Advisor Demo Response — Replace with a specific mini-audit

Current response to "Why aren't we growing?"
Visitors cannot quickly understand who you help and how. Your value is not clearly communicated above the fold. There is no clear next step guiding visitors to take action. Trust signals are limited, creating hesitation. SEO opportunities are missed that competitors capture.
Rewrite — show a real diagnostic
Based on a 3-second review of your current hero: your headline describes what you do, not what the visitor gets. "Tailored financial solutions" and "comprehensive services" appear in the first screen — both phrases your competitors use. The CTA says "Learn More" with no stated outcome. And the first number a visitor sees is your phone number, not a result. Here's the rewrite for your hero headline: [specific example].

That's the first 90 seconds of a full diagnostic. Would you like to see the rest?

Why: The current response is what every agency's AI chatbot says. The rewrite demonstrates the actual methodology — specific, named, with a rewrite — which is what Elliyeen sells. This is the proof of concept, not a description of it.

5. Founder Section Headline + Body

Current
"Most audits tell you what's wrong. They don't tell you what to do about it."

"I spent years watching businesses invest in websites that looked right but didn't convert..."
Rewrite
"I've sat across from founders who paid $40,000 for a website rebrand and saw no change in leads. The agency delivered a beautiful site. The conversion problem was never diagnosed."

"Elliyeen exists because the gap isn't design or copy — it's the diagnosis. When you know exactly what's costing you conversion, the fix is obvious. When you don't, you keep redesigning."

Why: The current copy is observational. The rewrite opens with a specific scene — a number, a situation, a failure. That is what makes a founder story credible and memorable. Halbert's internal monologue test: the reader who has paid for a website that didn't convert reads the new version and thinks "that's me."

6. Pricing — Add value anchor before the price

Current section opening
"Every engagement starts with a free 20-minute diagnostic. We look at your site for 20 minutes and give you the three highest-priority changes..."

Full Audit: From $1,500
Rewrite
"The last full audit we completed identified $36,600/month in uncaptured revenue for a single-location restaurant — without new ad spend. The audit that found it cost $1,500.

If we don't find at least five specific, actionable improvements on your site, the audit is free. We've never had to issue that refund."

Why: Kahneman's anchoring effect. The $36,600 number appears first and sets the reference frame. $1,500 against $36,600/month is not expensive — it is the lowest-risk purchase a founder can make. The guarantee then removes the remaining hesitation.

7. Referral Line — Elevate to a section

Current
Plain paragraph: "Know a founder whose site isn't converting? Send them this page."
Rewrite — give it a section
Know a founder stuck on this?
Every founder knows someone whose site looks fine but isn't converting. Forward this audit or share the page — if they book a diagnostic and mention your name, we'll run a 30-minute follow-up on your site at no charge.

[Forward this page →] [Copy the link]

Why: Cialdini reciprocity + word-of-mouth activation. The current version asks for a favor with no incentive. The rewrite gives a reason to refer and a mechanism to track it. This is the highest-ROI growth lever on the page and it currently has zero infrastructure.

8. FAQ — Add two missing objections

Missing Q5: "Why now? Can't I wait until after my next redesign?"

Recommended Answer
Most redesigns are built on the same positioning and copy that isn't converting — they just look better. The audit tells you what to fix before you commit to a rebuild. Clients who audit first rebuild once. Those who rebuild first often audit afterward and wish they'd done it in the other order.

Missing Q6: "Why not just hire a freelance copywriter or consultant?"

Recommended Answer
A copywriter improves the words. A UX consultant improves the layout. An SEO agency improves the rankings. None of them see the whole picture simultaneously. The reason most sites still don't convert after three agency engagements is that no one ran all the frameworks at once and ranked the findings by revenue impact. That's what this is.

Kahneman — Key Behavioral Gaps

  • Loss framing absent: Current copy uses gain framing ("get your free diagnosis," "know exactly what's costing you"). For a founder who has already spent on agencies that didn't deliver, loss framing converts better: "Every week without this diagnosis is a week of qualified visitors leaving for someone else."
  • No value anchor before price: The first number a visitor sees in the pricing section is "$1,500." Per anchoring theory, the first number sets the reference frame. Lead with "$36,600/month in uncaptured revenue" — then $1,500 is a bargain, not a cost.
  • Peak-end moment is undesigned: The most emotionally resonant moment on the page — the guarantee ("if we don't find five improvements, it's free") — is buried at the 25th percentile of attention. It should appear at peak attention (the pricing section headline) and be the last thing the visitor reads before the CTA.
  • WYSIATI gap: The page doesn't say how many audits have been completed. System 1 fills this void with "probably not many." A single number ("47 audits completed") closes this gap immediately.

Ogilvy Brand Analysis

The Big Idea

A Big Idea must pass Ogilvy's test: unique, durable, ownable, makes you gasp slightly, and could be used for 30 years.

The current site has the ingredients of a Big Idea but has not yet crystallized it into a single sentence:

Candidate Big Ideas:

  • "The only audit that gives you the fixed version, not just the problem." — Durable. Distinctive. No competitor says this.
  • "18 frameworks run simultaneously, so you don't have to hire 18 consultants." — Makes the value concrete. Specific.
  • "The audit that runs itself the way a McKinsey engagement would — on a founder's budget." — Positions against the highest-end alternative.

The first option is closest to what the site already proves with case studies. It should appear in the hero.

Manufacturer's Pride Count

Every sentence beginning with "We" or "Our" is a sentence that does not begin with the prospect. Count from the current page:

  • "We map your buyer's trigger moment..." → "You get a written customer brief that names your buyer's exact trigger."
  • "We run 18 specialist frameworks..." → "Your site gets 18 frameworks run simultaneously — copy, UX, SEO, trust, conversion, and 13 more."
  • "We stay until the system is working." → "You measure what moves. We stay until it does."
  • "We spent years watching businesses..." → Begin with the prospect's experience, not the founder's observation.

The reframe rule: every "we" sentence should become a "you" sentence. The prospect is the hero. Elliyeen is the guide.

The Burr — What Sticks

Ogilvy's burr is the single thing a visitor would repeat to a peer. The current candidate: "If we don't find five improvements, it's free." This is a strong burr — specific, surprising, memorable. It is currently buried. It should be the first line of the pricing section headline and appear in the hero subtext.
