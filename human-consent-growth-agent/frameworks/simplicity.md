# Simplicity

## Principle

Confused people delay. Remove every unnecessary decision, step, and word.

---

## How It Works

Cognitive load is the enemy of conversion. Every element that requires mental processing — every option, every unexplained term, every ambiguous instruction — consumes attention and increases the probability of abandonment.

The human brain under cognitive load does two things: makes worse decisions and defers decisions. Neither outcome serves voluntary participation.

The goal is not to simplify the offer. It is to simplify the path to understanding and choosing it.

---

## The Paradox of Choice

Barry Schwartz's research: increasing the number of options past a threshold reduces both satisfaction and the likelihood of choosing anything.

In practice:
- 3 options convert better than 10 in most contexts
- A single clear CTA converts better than 3 CTAs
- One focused page converts better than a navigation maze

Reduce options to the minimum required to serve the decision the prospect is actually making.

---

## Dimensions of Simplicity

### Copy Simplicity

Write at the level the audience actually reads, not at the level that signals sophistication.

Plain language principles:
- Active voice over passive
- Short sentences over long
- Concrete nouns over abstractions
- The audience's language over industry terminology
- Specific numbers over vague magnitudes ("17 families" over "many families")

Test: Can the primary message be understood on first read, in under 10 seconds?

---

### Process Simplicity

Every step the prospect must take should be obvious, necessary, and frictionless.

Friction audit:
- How many clicks to reach the primary CTA?
- How many form fields are required?
- How many pages load before the prospect sees value?
- How long does the response take after initial contact?

Remove every step that exists for the organization's convenience rather than the prospect's.

---

### Decision Simplicity

What is the one decision the prospect needs to make right now?

Not all decisions. Not the full commitment. The next single step.

"Book a 20-minute call" is simpler than "explore our care options."
"Download the guide" is simpler than "learn about our services."

The path to yes is built from a series of small, simple yeses — not one large complex commitment.

---

### Visual Simplicity

The eye needs a clear path. Every visual element competes for attention.

Principles:
- One primary action per visible screen
- High contrast for critical information
- White space is not wasted space — it is attention direction
- Remove decorative elements that do not communicate

If removing an element does not change what the prospect understands, remove it.

---

## The Simplicity Test

Read the page, email, or flow as if encountering it for the first time, knowing nothing about the organization.

Ask:
- What is being offered?
- Who is it for?
- What do I do next?

If any of these three questions require more than one reading, simplify.
