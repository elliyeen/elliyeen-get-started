# Authority

## Principle

Demonstrate expertise through evidence. Never manufacture authority.

---

## How It Works

Humans defer to experts when making high-stakes decisions in unfamiliar domains. Authority signals reduce perceived risk: "If this person knows what they're doing, my chances of a bad outcome go down."

The key word is "demonstrate." Authority must be earned and shown — not claimed, not implied.

---

## Types of Authority

### Earned Authority (Use Freely)

- **Credentials**: Licenses, certifications, degrees — verifiable by third parties
- **Track record**: Years of experience, documented outcomes, case studies
- **Published expertise**: Articles, guides, presentations that demonstrate knowledge
- **Institutional association**: Hospital network inclusion, industry association membership, accreditation
- **Recognition**: Awards, press mentions, peer endorsements

### Borrowed Authority (Use with Disclosure)

- **Expert endorsements**: Third-party experts who vouch for the offer
- **Institutional affiliation**: Association with trusted organizations
- **Media coverage**: Press mentions that validate quality

Borrowed authority requires disclosure of the relationship. Presenting an endorsement without revealing it was incentivized violates the no-fabrication standard.

### Manufactured Authority (Prohibited)

- **Fake credentials**: Claiming certifications not held
- **Inflated titles**: "Industry leader" without independent verification
- **False endorsements**: Endorsements that do not reflect genuine assessment
- **Fake press logos**: "As seen in" without actual coverage
- **Purchased reviews**: Reviews that do not reflect genuine customer experience

---

## Hopkins Application

Hopkins called this the Schlitz principle. Schlitz Beer's brewing process was the same as competitors'. Hopkins told the story of the process in detail — something no competitor had thought to publish. The audience assumed Schlitz was uniquely pure because they uniquely explained their standard.

The lesson: demonstrate what others leave invisible. Your process, your credentials, your standards — spelled out specifically — become authority assets.

---

## Building Authority in Practice

1. **Document the process.** Write down exactly how the work is done, step by step. Publish it.
2. **Name the credentials.** Not just "licensed" but "holds a current PHCP license, number [X], valid through [date]."
3. **Show the standards.** What does the organization refuse to do? What does it insist on? Named standards create authority.
4. **Create the record.** Compile outcomes with specificity. Not "we help families" but "17 families in Savannah found same-week placements through our intake process in the past 90 days."
5. **Let others validate.** Third-party reviews, partner endorsements, and press coverage carry more weight than self-assessment.

---

## The Authority Test

Before publishing any authority claim:
- Can this be verified by a skeptical third party?
- Would the credential body or institution confirm this affiliation?
- Is the scope of the credential accurately represented?

If any answer is no — revise, verify, or remove.
