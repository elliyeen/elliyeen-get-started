# Story

## Principle

Humans remember stories better than facts. Lead with narrative.

---

## How It Works

Facts activate the language-processing centers of the brain. Stories activate sensory, motor, and emotional regions — the brain "experiences" the story as if living it.

A fact tells: "Our caregivers are screened with background checks."
A story shows: "Maria had been with three agencies before. With each one, the caregiver arrived and her mother retreated to her room. With ours, the caregiver sat down at the kitchen table and asked her mother about the photographs. By the end of the first visit, her mother was telling stories she hadn't told in years. Maria cried on the drive home — not from exhaustion, from relief."

The fact is forgotten. The story is forwarded.

---

## Why Story Works

Jerome Bruner's research: people are 22 times more likely to remember information presented in story form than as isolated facts.

Paul Zak's research: stories with tension and resolution release oxytocin in the listener, increasing trust and generosity.

Narrative transportation: when a reader is absorbed in a story, their critical defenses lower and they evaluate information based on how it fits the story rather than against external criteria.

---

## Story as the Bridge

Eugene Schwartz's insight: people buy movement from a current state to a desired state.

Story makes this movement visible:
- **Current state**: the fear, the friction, the exhaustion, the uncertainty
- **The bridge**: the offer, described as an event rather than a product
- **Desired state**: the relief, the peace, the new normal

The prospect reading the story sees their own current state reflected, then sees the bridge, then sees the future they want. The story does the selling.

---

## Story in Practice

### In Headlines

A story headline implies a narrative arc:

"She called three agencies. Ours was the only one that knew her mother's name before the first visit."

This headline contains: a search (three agencies), a standard (knowing the name), a result (implied: better outcome). The story begins before the first paragraph.

---

### In Testimonials

Transform reviews into narratives:

Not: "Great service, very professional."

Yes: "I was managing everything from Atlanta — my mom's medications, her appointments, her loneliness. I found Shannon's number at 11pm on a Thursday. By Saturday, her caregiver was there. My mom called me that Sunday to tell me about her new friend. That was six months ago. I sleep now."

The second version contains: situation, action, outcome, transformation. It is memorable and shareable.

---

### In the About Page

The founder story is the organization's most valuable story asset.

It must answer:
- What did you experience that made this necessary?
- What did you see that others weren't addressing?
- Why does this matter to you personally?

Generic: "We are passionate about helping families."
Story: "I was the CNA in the room. I saw what happened when a family got the wrong caregiver. I saw what happened when they got the right one. I built this agency because I knew what the difference felt like — and I wanted every family to feel the right version."

---

## Story Guardrails

- All stories must be true or labeled as illustrative
- Quotes must be exact and consented
- Outcomes described must be achievable, not exceptional edge cases presented as typical
- No composite characters presented as real individuals
