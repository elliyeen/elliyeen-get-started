# Repetition

## Principle

Familiarity builds trust. Show up consistently, over time, across channels.

---

## How It Works

The mere exposure effect (Robert Zajonc, 1968): people develop a preference for things they have encountered repeatedly, even without conscious awareness.

Repetition does several things simultaneously:
- Builds brand recall (the name comes to mind when the need arises)
- Creates familiarity (the known feels safer than the unknown)
- Signals stability (organizations that have been consistently present are perceived as reliable)
- Reinforces key messages (repeated claims are more readily believed than single claims)

---

## The Awareness Timeline

Most high-consideration purchases involve multiple exposures before a decision is made.

Research suggests 7–12 exposures before a prospect moves toward a decision in low-trust categories. In high-trust categories (medical, financial, legal), the number is higher.

This means:
- First impression is rarely the decision point
- The organization that stays consistently present wins the decision at the moment it arrives
- Abandoning a channel after a short period resets the accumulation

---

## Repetition Strategy

### Channel Repetition

Maintain a consistent presence on high-value channels rather than sporadic presence on many channels.

Better: weekly presence on 2 channels for 12 months.
Worse: daily presence on 8 channels for 3 weeks.

The audience must encounter the brand repeatedly before they encounter it at the moment of decision.

---

### Message Repetition

Core messages must be repeated across formats, not constantly refreshed.

The organization's positioning, its key differentiator, its central story — these should appear consistently, not be retired for novelty.

New content: encouraged.
New positioning: only when genuinely earned.

---

### Timing Repetition

Be present on a predictable schedule.

- Weekly email: arrives every Tuesday morning
- Monthly guide: published the first Friday of the month
- Quarterly check-in with referral partners: consistent

Predictability is itself a trust signal. The audience learns to expect — and then look for — the consistent presence.

---

## The Repetition Trap

Repetition of bad content accelerates negative perception.

Repetition amplifies whatever signal is being sent. If the signal is quality and genuine helpfulness, repetition builds trust. If the signal is noise, self-promotion, or empty content, repetition builds resentment.

Before scaling repetition, confirm the content is worth repeating.

---

## Practical Application

Starting from zero:
1. Choose 2 channels where the target audience is active
2. Commit to a specific cadence (weekly, biweekly)
3. Hold the cadence for 90 days without measuring results
4. Review: what is getting engagement? What is being ignored?
5. Double down on what is working. Do not change channels yet.

The goal of the first 90 days is presence, not performance. Performance compounds from presence.
