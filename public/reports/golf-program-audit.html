The recruiting pages had the standard elements: roster, schedule, facility photos, coaching staff, team history. The copy was accurate. It was well-produced. And it was aimed at the wrong reader.

It was written for a parent who wants to see legitimacy. It was read by a 17-year-old athlete whose actual question — "Can I get playing time here and is there money?" — went unanswered on every page.

The Actual Buyer

A Division I golf recruit is not evaluating the program's pedigree. By the time they reach your recruiting page, they know your tournament record. They found you because a coach reached out, a parent googled, or a recruiting service surfaced the name. Legitimacy is already established.

"The recruit's core question is never 'Is this program good?' It's 'Can I play here? Will I get a scholarship? What happens to my career if I choose this school and it doesn't work out?'"

These are not questions the standard program page answers. They are the questions that determine whether a recruit visits — or commits to someone else who made them feel less afraid.

The Schwartz principle at work: People buy movement — from where they are now to where they want to be. The recruit is in the current state: uncertain, under pressure, aware that four years of eligibility is not recoverable. The recruiting page needs to sell the bridge to the desired state: confidence that this is the right choice and that their career is safe here.

Finding 1 — The Core Fear Was Never Named

The single greatest hesitation a recruit carries is the fear of choosing wrong. Not wrong as in losing — wrong as in wasting eligibility at a program where they never get to play at their level, then running out of years to transfer and find the right fit.

This fear exists in every recruiting conversation. It is the subtext of every "I want to visit a few more schools" delay. It is never named on any recruiting page in this tier — and that silence creates anxiety rather than confidence.

What the page said

  • Team GPA and community service
  • Conference championships
  • Coaching philosophy (generic)
  • Facility square footage

What the recruit needed

  • Roster turnover — who is graduating, who has remaining eligibility
  • Average playing time for incoming freshmen
  • What happens if the fit isn't right
  • Stories from current players who made this decision
The fix
Name the fear. Then dissolve it with specifics.
One paragraph. Direct acknowledgment that choosing a program is one of the biggest decisions of a young athlete's life. Then: specific graduation data, transfer portal transparency, and a player quote that speaks to the decision — not to the program's trophies. The recruit who sees their fear acknowledged stops looking elsewhere.

Finding 2 — The Stats Page Answered the Wrong Question

The program's stats page was designed to demonstrate competitive success. Tournament wins, scoring average, conference finishes. All accurate. None of it was what a recruit searched for when they opened the page.

A recruit looking at the stats page is asking one question: Can I actually play here? That means: What is the scoring differential between your current players and my current ranking? Where does my AJGA record fit in your lineup? Who leaves this year and when would I compete for their spot?

The problem
Stats built for a media guide. Not for a recruiting decision.
Team scoring average. Wins and losses. National rankings. These numbers answer "how good is the program?" — a question the recruit already knows the answer to before they arrive at the page. The numbers that would actually move a recruiting decision were absent entirely.
The rebuild
Roster context that answers "where would I fit?"
Add one section: "Where you'd fit." Roster positions by class year, current scoring range for lineup spots 1–5, and two or three recent incoming recruit profiles (anonymized or named with permission) showing where they came from and where they competed in year one. This is the information that converts a visit request into a commitment conversation.

Finding 3 — Scholarship Language Was Completely Absent

"Golf scholarships Division I" is the most-searched phrase among prospects researching this program's tier. Zero scholarship-related content appeared anywhere on the recruiting pages, the athletics FAQ, or the program homepage.

The gap
Most-searched topic. Completely unaddressed.
Recruits and their parents search for scholarship information before they reach out to a coach. When a program's site has no scholarship content, the prospect does not assume scholarships don't exist — they assume the program isn't worth pursuing, or they move on to a school that answers the question. The absence of information reads as indifference to the recruit's financial reality.
NCAA equivalency context: Division I golf is an equivalency sport — programs can divide scholarship value across multiple athletes. Most recruits and parents don't know this. Explaining it positions the program as transparent and recruit-friendly before the first conversation with a coach.
The fix
One honest paragraph about how golf scholarships work at this program.
Not a commitment. Not a promise. An explanation: how Division I golf scholarships function, what the program offers, and how to start the conversation. Programs that explain the process before a recruit asks for it are perceived as transparent. Transparency reduces anxiety. Reduced anxiety produces committed recruits who stop shopping.

Recommended Path

Three additions. All within the existing site structure. Each one addresses a specific decision-gap that was costing recruiting conversations.

Priority 1 — Recruiting page hero
Acknowledge the decision. Don't just describe the program.
One headline rewrite. From program-focused ("A tradition of excellence") to recruit-focused ("You have four years. Here's how to make sure you pick right."). The recruit who feels understood stays on the page. The recruit who sees a brochure clicks back.
Priority 2 — Stats page
Add a "Roster Outlook" section with lineup context
Class year breakdown, graduation timeline for the next two years, and current lineup scoring range. This is not sensitive information — it is public record. Publishing it directly removes the ambiguity that causes recruits to delay decisions while they try to assess fit indirectly through coaches.
Priority 3 — New scholarship FAQ section
Explain how scholarships work before recruits have to ask
Three to five questions: How does Division I golf scholarships work? Does this program offer full scholarships? When do scholarship conversations happen in the recruiting process? What academic merit aid is available alongside athletic aid? Programs that answer these questions publicly shorten the recruiting timeline and attract recruits whose families have already self-selected for financial fit.
