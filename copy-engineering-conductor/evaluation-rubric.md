# Evaluation Rubric

Use these questions to evaluate each behavioral force. Score after answering — not before.

---

## Force 1: Attention

**Core question:** Would the prospect stop?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the opening line target the exact prospect or everyone?
2. Does the headline make a specific promise, name a specific pain, or create a specific curiosity gap?
3. Would a person in this situation, with this awareness level, continue reading after the first line?
4. Is the opening line generic enough to be written by anyone, or specific enough to belong to only this offer?
5. Does the visual hierarchy (if applicable) direct the eye to the most behaviorally important element first?

### Failure Patterns

- "Welcome to [Company]" — announces the sender, not the reader's situation
- Generic benefit headlines ("Achieve more. Live better.") — could apply to anything
- Clever wordplay that requires the prospect to interpret before they care
- Long subheadlines doing the work the headline should do

### Strong Patterns

- Naming the prospect's exact situation in the first line
- Making a claim so specific it could only apply to a narrow audience
- Opening with the fear or desire the prospect woke up with
- A number or fact that reframes the prospect's understanding of their situation

---

## Force 2: Curiosity

**Core question:** Would they want to know more?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the copy create an open loop the prospect wants to close?
2. Is there a specific claim, mechanism, or insight the reader can't fully evaluate without reading further?
3. Does each section pull the reader into the next?
4. Is there a stated or implied "and here's why" that the reader must get to?
5. Does the copy withhold something the right prospect would want to know?

### Failure Patterns

- All information delivered upfront — nothing to discover
- Curiosity gaps that are too vague ("you won't believe what happened")
- Curiosity that's built for the wrong audience
- The reader can fully evaluate the offer without reading further

### Strong Patterns

- "Here's what most [role] don't realize about [topic]..."
- A counterintuitive claim that requires explanation
- A process or mechanism that sounds different from what the prospect has tried
- A specific result that prompts the question "how?"

---

## Force 3: Understanding

**Core question:** Do they instantly understand what is being offered?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Could the prospect explain the offer to someone else after one reading?
2. Is the offer (what you get, for whom, at what cost, and with what next step) stated clearly?
3. Is there any jargon, internal language, or abstraction that requires the prospect to translate?
4. Is the CTA connected to the offer in an obvious way?
5. Would a distracted person reading on a phone in 30 seconds know what to do?

### Failure Patterns

- Offer buried below the fold
- CTA disconnected from the offer ("Learn more" when the offer is a free audit)
- Technical terms used without definition
- Copy that builds to the offer rather than leading with it
- Multiple offers competing for the same action

### Strong Patterns

- Offer stated in the headline or opening paragraph
- One CTA, one next step, one offer per page
- Plain language that matches how the prospect would describe their problem
- Clarity about what happens after the click

---

## Force 4: Desire

**Core question:** Does this connect to what they deeply want?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the copy name the current state the prospect wants to escape?
2. Does the copy paint the desired state the prospect wants to reach?
3. Is the desired state specific to this audience, or generic?
4. Does the copy sell the movement (from state A to state B), not just the product?
5. Is there an emotional layer below the functional benefit?

### Failure Patterns

- Listing features without connecting them to the prospect's situation
- Desired state is generic ("save time," "grow your business")
- Current state is mentioned but not felt — stated clinically
- No bridge between the offer and the prospect's life after the purchase

### Strong Patterns

- "You're ___. You want to be ___. Here's the bridge."
- Specific future state painted in a scene the prospect can picture
- Current pain named in the prospect's own language
- Emotional resonance beneath the functional benefit

---

## Force 5: Belief

**Core question:** Do they believe the promise can happen?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. What is the core claim of this copy?
2. What evidence exists to support it?
3. Is the evidence specific enough to be evaluated (named client, real number, verifiable result)?
4. Is the mechanism explained — why does this work, not just that it works?
5. Is the evidence relevant to this prospect's situation, or too generic to apply?

### Failure Patterns

- Claims without evidence ("Our clients see dramatic results")
- Vague social proof ("Thousands of satisfied customers")
- Results that are possible but not made believable by the copy
- No mechanism — the claim is stated but not made understandable

### Strong Patterns

- Named client + specific outcome + time frame
- Before/after comparison that mirrors the prospect's situation
- Mechanism explanation ("Here's why it works even if you've tried X before")
- Third-party validation from a source the prospect already trusts

---

## Force 6: Trust

**Core question:** Do they trust the source, proof, and process?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the reader know who is making this offer?
2. Is there a reason to trust this specific person or company over alternatives?
3. Are trust signals relevant to what the prospect fears most?
4. Is there any element that would raise a red flag or trigger skepticism?
5. Does the copy feel like it is serving the prospect or selling to them?

### Failure Patterns

- Anonymous offer ("A team of experts")
- Generic testimonials with no name, role, or result
- Claims that are too good to be believed
- Aggressive urgency or pressure tactics
- Legal disclaimers that undercut the promise

### Strong Patterns

- Named founder with relevant credential
- Testimonials with name, role, and specific result
- Third-party logos, media mentions, or certifications
- Process transparency ("Here's exactly what happens after you click")
- Acknowledgment of what the offer cannot or does not do

---

## Force 7: Friction Reduction

**Core question:** What makes action feel risky, hard, expensive, confusing, or delayed?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. What is the prospect's biggest objection to acting right now?
2. Is that objection addressed directly in the copy?
3. What is the cost (time, money, risk, social) of taking the next step?
4. Is the next step small enough relative to the perceived value?
5. Are there any unnecessary steps between desire and action?

### Failure Patterns

- Objections unaddressed or dismissed
- "No obligation" said without proof
- Process unclear — prospect doesn't know what happens after they click
- Action requires too much commitment for the current trust level
- Urgency manufactured without reason ("Limited time offer" with no reason)

### Strong Patterns

- "Here's what happens after you click": specific process, timeline, expectation
- Risk reversal: guarantee, free trial, no-commitment first step
- Objection named and dissolved before the CTA
- Lowering the cost of the first action to match the current level of trust

---

## Force 8: Action

**Core question:** Is the next step obvious and compelling?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Is there a single, clear next step?
2. Does the CTA sell the value of taking the action — not just describe it?
3. Is the CTA connected to the desire the copy just created?
4. Does the reader know exactly what happens after they click?
5. Is the CTA placed where the reader's desire peaks?

### Failure Patterns

- Generic CTAs ("Learn more," "Click here," "Submit")
- Multiple CTAs competing for the same attention
- CTA placed before desire is established
- CTA restates the offer rather than selling the next step
- Action requires too much from the reader at this trust level

### Strong Patterns

- CTA describes what the reader gets, not what they do ("Get your free audit" vs. "Book now")
- CTA placed immediately after the strongest belief or desire moment
- One primary CTA per page or section
- CTA anticipates the objection ("No credit card required")

---

## Force 9: Loyalty

**Core question:** Does the message make the relationship feel valuable beyond the first transaction?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the copy signal that this relationship has ongoing value?
2. Is there a reason the prospect would want to stay connected after the first action?
3. Does the copy treat the prospect as someone worth a long relationship, or just a lead?
4. Is there anything in the copy that would make a customer feel seen and served over time?
5. Does the copy position the provider as a long-term resource, not just a one-time solution?

### Failure Patterns

- Copy ends at the CTA with nothing about what comes next
- Entire message is transactional — the relationship disappears after the click
- No signal of community, continued value, or ongoing support
- Copy treats the reader as a buyer, not a person

### Strong Patterns

- References to what happens after the first step
- Signal of community or ongoing relationship
- Copy that positions the brand as a trusted resource over time
- Language that treats the reader as someone worth a long-term relationship

---

## Force 10: Advocacy

**Core question:** Would the prospect feel proud to share, refer, or recommend this?

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Does the copy contain a specific insight or truth the prospect would want to pass on?
2. Is there a positioning statement, claim, or framing that is unusual enough to be shared?
3. Would a prospect feel comfortable sending this to a peer who has the same problem?
4. Does the copy validate the prospect's worldview in a way they'd want to amplify?
5. Is there anything in the copy worth repeating?

### Failure Patterns

- Generic copy that could have been written by any competitor
- No specific insight, original claim, or memorable line
- Copy that is accurate but not remarkable
- Copy so salesy the prospect would be embarrassed to share it

### Strong Patterns

- A counterintuitive insight the prospect wants to share
- A specific framing of the problem that makes the prospect feel understood
- A positioning claim that validates the prospect's experience
- A result or story worth repeating

---

## Conducting the Evaluation

Work through all ten force sections. Answer the diagnostic questions first. Let the answers determine the score — not the other way around.

After scoring all ten forces, identify the single force whose low score most prevents the desired action. That is the bottleneck.

Recommend one fix for the bottleneck only.
