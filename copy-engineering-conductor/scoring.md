# Scoring Rules

Each of the ten behavioral forces is scored 0–10.

The overall score is the average of all ten force scores, expressed as a value out of 100.

---

## Score Bands

### 0–3: Weak

The copy does not create meaningful movement on this force.

The prospect's state is unchanged. This force is not functioning.

Examples:
- Attention 2: The headline does not stop anyone who is not already searching for this
- Belief 1: A major claim is made with no evidence of any kind
- Action 0: There is no CTA or next step present in the copy

---

### 4–6: Functional

The copy is understandable but not persuasive enough to drive movement.

The force is present but not doing enough behavioral work.

Examples:
- Curiosity 5: The offer is clear but the reader feels no pull to know more
- Trust 4: No red flags, but no reasons to believe either
- Friction 6: The offer is free, which helps, but the process feels uncertain

---

### 7–8: Strong

The copy creates clear movement toward the desired behavior on this force.

The prospect's state changes meaningfully after encountering this.

Examples:
- Desire 8: The copy names a specific pain that lands for this audience, and paints a believable future state
- Understanding 7: Offer and next step are immediately clear with no re-reading required
- Action 8: CTA names exactly what happens next and why it's worth doing

---

### 9–10: Exceptional

The copy makes the next action feel natural, believable, and desirable on this force.

The force is fully activated. It is unlikely that a stronger version of this element could be written for this audience.

Examples:
- Attention 10: The opening line stops the exact prospect and creates immediate identification
- Belief 9: Proof is specific, named, and directly relevant to the prospect's situation
- Trust 10: Founder credential, client results, and third-party validation are all present and specific

---

## Per-Force Scoring Anchors

### Attention

- 0–3: Headline would be ignored by the target audience
- 4–6: Headline is relevant but not arresting
- 7–8: Headline stops the right reader and creates immediate identification
- 9–10: Headline is so specific to this prospect's situation they feel it was written for them

### Curiosity

- 0–3: Reader has no reason to continue after the opening
- 4–6: Reader understands the offer but feels no pull to discover more
- 7–8: Reader wants to know: how, who, and whether this applies to them
- 9–10: Reader cannot stop without resolving the tension the copy created

### Understanding

- 0–3: Offer or next step is unclear or requires re-reading
- 4–6: Offer is eventually understood but requires effort
- 7–8: Offer and next step are immediately obvious
- 9–10: Offer is so clear the reader could explain it to someone else after one pass

### Desire

- 0–3: Copy does not connect to anything the prospect deeply wants
- 4–6: Copy mentions the right topic but doesn't create emotional pull
- 7–8: Copy names the pain and the desired state in language the prospect recognizes
- 9–10: Copy makes the desired future feel real, close, and worth acting for

### Belief

- 0–3: Copy makes claims without any evidence; prospect has no reason to believe
- 4–6: Copy implies results but doesn't substantiate them specifically
- 7–8: Copy includes specific, plausible evidence the prospect can evaluate
- 9–10: Proof is so specific and relevant that skepticism would require active effort

### Trust

- 0–3: No signals of credibility; source is unknown or unvalidated
- 4–6: Copy implies legitimacy but provides no verifiable trust signals
- 7–8: Name, credential, result, or third-party validation is present and specific
- 9–10: Multiple trust signals are layered and relevant to this specific prospect's fears

### Friction Reduction

- 0–3: Action feels risky, expensive, confusing, or requiring significant commitment
- 4–6: Risk is partially addressed but the reader still has unresolved hesitations
- 7–8: Objections are anticipated and dissolved before they become blockers
- 9–10: Action feels like the obvious next step — lower risk than not acting

### Action

- 0–3: No clear CTA, or CTA is vague / buried / uninspiring
- 4–6: CTA exists but does not sell the value of taking the action
- 7–8: CTA is clear, specific, and connected to the prospect's desire
- 9–10: CTA makes the next step feel both obvious and desirable — reader knows exactly what to do and why

### Loyalty

- 0–3: Copy creates no sense of ongoing relationship or value beyond the transaction
- 4–6: Copy implies continued value but doesn't make it feel real or worth staying for
- 7–8: Copy signals that the relationship will continue to serve the prospect's interests
- 9–10: Copy makes the reader feel that the relationship itself is worth having — not just the product

### Advocacy

- 0–3: Nothing in the copy would cause the reader to share, refer, or recommend
- 4–6: Copy is likable but not remarkable or shareable
- 7–8: Copy contains a specific insight or positioning that the reader would want to pass on
- 9–10: Copy contains a truth so useful or precise that the reader wants to share it with someone in a similar situation

---

## Calculating the Overall Score

Sum all ten force scores. Multiply by 1. This produces a score out of 100.

Example:
- Attention: 7
- Curiosity: 6
- Understanding: 8
- Desire: 6
- Belief: 3
- Trust: 3
- Friction Reduction: 6
- Action: 6
- Loyalty: 2
- Advocacy: 2

Sum: 49
Multiplied by 1: **49 / 100**

---

## Score Interpretation

| Range | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0–39 | Moves Away — structural failure |
| 40–59 | Neutral — not doing enough behavioral work |
| 60–74 | Moves Closer — functional but has a clear bottleneck |
| 75–89 | Moves Closer — strong; refine the bottleneck only |
| 90–100 | Exceptional — rare; verify before shipping |

---

## Notes on Weighting

All forces are scored equally in the base system.

However, the **bottleneck principle** applies: a single critical failure (Belief at 1, Action at 0) can block movement even when all other forces score well. The conductor must identify this regardless of the average score.

A copy scoring 72/100 with Action at 0 is effectively broken — the score does not override the bottleneck analysis.
